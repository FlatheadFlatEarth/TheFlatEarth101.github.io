## The Sun's Path

On a Flat Earth, the Sun is much nearer to Earth and much smaller than in the heliocentric model. Numbers vary, but it seems that the Sun is much closer than the 93 million miles embraced by the heliocentric model. The Sun follows a circular path above the Earth, tracing the line of the Equator during the equinoxes, the Tropic of Cancer during the Summer Solstice (in the northern hemisphere), and the Tropic of Capricorn during the Winter Solstice.

{{< figure src="/images/sun/tropics.jpg" title="" width=600 >}}

[Photo Credit: Ashlee Webster](https://flatgeocentricearth.wordpress.com/2015/12/03/seasons-on-the-flat-earth-2/)

This leads to the seasons. Depending on the time of year, the Sun will be more or less proximate to different areas on the Earth. The closer the Sun, the warmer it will be. The further, the colder.

## Day/night Pattern 

Each day, the Sun flies a 360-degree circle over the Earth. 

Wherever the Sun is currently over, it is **12 noon** at that location.

Wherever the Sun is 180-degrees-across-from, it is **midnight** at that location.

As the Sun flies towards and then past you, you will perceive the day as passing.

When the Sun is far enough away, you will no longer be lit and warmed by its rays, and darkness will fall upon you.

### Light Propagation

One common argument against the Local Sun is that, even with a smaller sun, "the entire Earth should all be daytime at once, because there would be nothing to block the Sun's rays."

However, this is not true. Both globe scientists and Flat Earthers agree that light has a limited ability to penetrate through the atmosphere.

The same way that your headlights can't penetrate through fog, the Sun can only cast its rays through through so much atmosphere. 

When its rays stop penetrating the atmosphere, the land beyond its range is shrouded in darkness.

{{< figure src="/images/physics/light-prop/smoke-flashlight.jpg" title="" width=700 >}}

This same effect can be replicated at home using a flashlight and a variety of atmospheric conditions.

If you live in an area prone to forest fires, you are likely already familiar with how different the sky, distant landscape, and celestial bodies are when smoke is in the air. If fog is common in your area, you are almost certainly familiar with the light-hampering effects of fog.
If the humidity level swings between dry and wet, you will also see an effect.
If you live near an industrial site which kicks up particles into the air, you should be able to tell a difference between when the jobsite is active versus idle.

For those of a more scholarly leaning, here is a paper which examines the effects of various atmospheric particles on the transmission of both optical (light) and infrared (heat) waves through the atmosphere.

["Propagation of optical and infrared waves in the atmosphere"](/images/papers/light-prop/Nabousi.pdf) by Al Naboulsi, et al.  
[Original URL](https://ursi.org/proceedings/procGA05/pdf/F01P.7(01729).pdf)

Unlike what you were taught in school, the Earth is not the only thing that can stop the Sun's rays.

### Arctic Temperatures

Temperatures at the North Pole can be hard to come by, because, according to the mainstream view, the area surrounding the north pole is ocean, with occasional ice coverage. Thus, it's much more difficult to establish a permanent research base at the North Pole.

Luckily, there are buoy-based and satellite-based temperature readings available. In my research, I came across a dataset provided by a team at the University of Washington's Polar Science Center, led by [Ignatius Rigor](http://psc.apl.uw.edu/people/investigators/ignatius-rigor/).

In their publication, [ARCSS Surface Air Temperature (ARCSS-SAT) Analysis](https://data.eol.ucar.edu/dataset/106.308), the team positions their dataset as a "best estimate of surface air temperature (SAT) over the Arctic Ocean in 12-hourly gridded fields from 1979-2008," also noting that, "This analysis adjusts a warm bias exhibited by [the prior analysis] in comparison to manned drifting station data over the Arctic Ocean, and improves the IABP/POLES SAT analysis." So, in this dataset, the Arctic region is actually colder in comparison to the prior dataset.

Relative coldness aside, you will see that the temperatures at the Northern Polar Region (in this dataset, the northernmost region is marked as 88.542 degrees north. The southernmost is at -88.542. It's unclear if these are the extreme boundaries or if the granularity of the dataset actually means that these measurements include the poles at 90 degrees.) are significantly warmer than those reported at the South Pole.

Antarctic temperatures ranged from approximately -58C to -24C.
Arctic temperatures ranged from approximately -35C to 0C.

What could cause this?

Many Flat Earthers believe that this difference is caused by Antarctica/the "South Pole" being further from the Sun than the North Pole, and thus, further from the warming rays of the Sun.
