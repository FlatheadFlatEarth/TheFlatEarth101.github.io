The Sun's Path

On a Flat Earth, the Sun is much nearer to Earth and much smaller than in the heliocentric model. Numbers vary, but it seems that the Sun is much closer than the 93 million miles embraced by the heliocentric model. The Sun follows a circular path above the Earth, tracing the line of the Equator during the equinoxes, the Tropic of Cancer during the Summer Solstice (in the northern hemisphere), and the Tropic of Capricorn during the Winter Solstice.

Photo Credit: Ashlee Webster

This leads to the seasons. Depending on the time of year, the Sun will be more or less proximate to different areas on the Earth. The closer the Sun, the warmer it will be. The further, the colder.

Day/night Pattern

Each day, the Sun flies a 360-degree circle over the Earth.

Wherever the Sun is currently over, it is 12 noon at that location.

Wherever the Sun is 180-degrees-across-from, it is midnight at that location.

As the Sun flies towards and then past you, you will perceive the day as passing.

When the Sun is far enough away, you will no longer be lit and warmed by its rays, and darkness will fall upon you.

Light Propagation

One common argument against the Local Sun is that, "the entire Earth should all be daytime at once."

However, this is not true. Both globe scientists and Flat Earthers agree that light has a limited ability to penetrate through the atmosphere.

The same way that your headlights can't penetrate through fog, the Sun can only cast its rays through through so much atmosphere.

When its rays stop penetrating the atmosphere, the land beyond its range is shrouded in darkness.

This same effect can be replicated at home using a flashlight and a variety of atmospheric conditions.

If you live in an area prone to forest fires, you are likely already familiar with how different the sky, distant landscape, and celestial bodies are when smoke is in the air. If fog is common in your area, you are almost certainly familiar with the light-hampering effects of fog. If the humidity level swings between dry and wet, you will also see an effect. If you live near an industrial site which kicks up particles into the air, you should be able to tell a difference between when the jobsite is active versus idle.

For those of a more scholarly leaning, here is a paper which examines the effects of various atmospheric particles on the transmission of both optical (light) and infrared (heat) waves through the atmosphere.

"Propagation of optical and infrared waves in the atmosphere" by Al Naboulsi, et al.

Unlike what you were taught in school, the Earth is not the only thing that can stop the Sun's rays.

Sun Over Water Creates a Hot-spot

Next time that you are looking out at the ocean, pay attention to the surface of the water. You will notice that there is a hot spot beneath the Sun's current position.

"Ocean Afternoon" by microwavenby is licensed under CC BY-NC 2.0

Is this consistent with an object sitting at 90+ million miles away?

Or is this consistent with a local Sun?

Fan-out of rays through the clouds

You've likely also seen the Sun's rays fanning out as they pass through the clouds.

Photo Credit

Is this a dramatic refractive effect, causing the 90-million-mile-away Sun's nearly-parallel rays to drastically alter their paths, shooting outwards?

Or is this more consistent with a local Sun, whose diverging rays are being highlit by passing through the haze of the atmosphere?

Large Discrepancy Between Arctic and Antarctic Temperatures

On a spherical Earth, should not the North and South Poles enjoy very similar climates to one another?

If the axial tilt is the reason for the seasons, and the equator receives even sunlight all year round, what reason would there be for the South Pole to be much colder than the North Pole?

Arctic Temperatures

Temperatures at the North Pole can be hard to come by, because, according to the mainstream view, the area surrounding the north pole is ocean, with occasional ice coverage. Thus, it's much more difficult to establish a permanent research base at the North Pole.

Luckily, there are buoy-based and satellite-based temperature readings available. In my research, I came across a dataset provided by a team at the University of Washington's Polar Science Center, led by Ignatius Rigor.

In their publication, ARCSS Surface Air Temperature (ARCSS-SAT) Analysis, the team positions their dataset as a "best estimate of surface air temperature (SAT) over the Arctic Ocean in 12-hourly gridded fields from 1979-2008," also noting that, "This analysis adjusts a warm bias exhibited by [the prior analysis] in comparison to manned drifting station data over the Arctic Ocean, and improves the IABP/POLES SAT analysis." So, in this dataset, the Arctic region is actually colder in comparison to the prior dataset.

Relative coldness aside, you will see that the temperatures at the Northern Polar Region (in this dataset, the northernmost region is marked as 88.542 degrees north. The southernmost is at -88.542. It's unclear if these are the extreme boundaries or if the granularity of the dataset actually means that these measurements include the poles at 90 degrees.) are significantly warmer than those reported at the South Pole.

Antarctic temperatures ranged from approximately -58C to -24C. Arctic temperatures ranged from approximately -35C to 0C.

What could cause this?

Many Flat Earthers believe that this difference is caused by Antarctica/the "South Pole" being further from the Sun than the North Pole, and thus, further from the warming rays of the Sun.
